Fostering ecological perception:

A temporal remoteness re-cartography of Venice's lagoon morphological development

ABOUT THE PROJECT

"Maps have always been problematic for cartographic research because they rarely allow for ambivalence or contradictions, despite their interpretative nature. Maps have promoted a universal and immutable version of the earth’s surface, encouraging a perceptual detachment of each environment. Critical cartography and counter-mapping theories have been a meaningful effort to question the dominant cartographic procedures, making domination visible and providing alternative realities of the territories. However, they still visualize the landscapes as a knowable world, repeating established mapping and cartographic orthodoxies. Therefore, How can communication design encourage alternative cartographic procedures, telling ecological narratives?"

“Fostering ecological perception: a temporal remoteness re-cartography of Venice’s lagoon morphological development” is my Double Degree thesis written at Politecnico di Milano and Tongji University. The dissertation explores the role communication design in enabling novel spatial representations of the territory through its semiotic approach and its ability to envision new narratives. Therefore, I explored the role of scientific cartography and alternative cartography in the perception of the territory. The thesis provides a theoretical and practical contribute offering an alternative cartography of Venice's lagoon.

KNOWING PHASE

In the first part of the research, starting from the considerations of Capra and Luisi who stated "the environmental crisis can be largely perceived as a crisis of perception (Capra, Luisi, 2014)", and Val Plumwood theory of ecological perception crisis patterns, I framed the theoretical foundation of the research, tackling one of hidden ecological perception patterns: temporal remoteness, i.e. temporal distance from cause and effects. Then, I investigated literature review from Cartography, Critical cartography, and Counter cartography to discuss the role of maps as tools that perpetuate a snapshot of the territory as fixed, knowable and linear.

ANALYZING PHASE

In the second phase of the research, I analyzed the Venice’s lagoon context and history with the aim of provide a contextual framework for the research. Combining a literature review from Venice’s lagoon morphological development, research desk, and a fieldwork, I got information about the morphological composition of Venice’s lagoon, the primary human intervention from XIX to XX century and the main morphological consequence caused by these interventions. This phase was essential to gain an interpretative understanding of Venice's lagoon necessary for the design phase

DESIGNING PHASE

In the third phase of the research, I presented a design challenge on Venice's lagoon morphological development. I designed a temporal remoteness re-cartography of Venice's lagoon, following the approach and method of re-cartography. Deconstructing Venice's lagoon historical cartography, the challenge was to construct novel maps of Venice's lagoon morphological development, dismantling the "view from above" orthodoxy to encourage a horizontal perspective of the lagoon, able to give a contextual perception of an aquatic territory.

A STILL GOING PROCESS

The ultimate goal of the project was to prototype a digital atlas of Venice's lagoon morphological development. Currently I am still working on my thesis. I chose to give up the digital atlas idea because I no longer believe in its effectiveness. The atlas is the ideal container for such a project, but a digital medium is not able to perceptually stimulate a user as much as creating a physical experience. I am currently pursuing this path with the goal that my project, which I believe in very much, can find a window to reach a wide audience.
